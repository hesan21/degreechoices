Progressivism and Academia:
The Most Progressive Universities in the US

What is the most progressive university in the US? It’s a question that’s often asked but rarely answered in any satisfactory way. Although the question of progressivism is open ended and subjective to some extent, we have endeavored to define the most progressive institutions based on a variety of metrics.

Findings

The California Institute of Technology (Caltech) narrowly edged out New York University as the most diverse college in the US. Its score of 58.59% was thanks, in part, to its current diversity policy and, in turn, the demographics of its student body. As per its Center for Inclusion and Diversity program, Caltech abides by state and federal laws, including Title IX, at all levels. This includes students, faculty, researchers, and staff. This commitment to diversity has led to a student population that’s 54.7% men and 45.3% women. Of the 2,237 undergraduate and graduate students, 27% are white. Overall, seven racial-ethnic groups are represented at Caltech, with Asian and black students accounting for a total of 53.8% of the student population. In contrast, the University of Notre Dame, which had the lowest diversity score (10.81%), has a white student population of 63.5%. It also scored poorly with regards to geographic diversity (11.85%) and the number of LGBTQ students (13.89%). For those reasons, Notre Dame was ranked as the lowest US university of overall diversity.

Findings

The desire to achieve a diverse environment is important. However, this is only possible if the financial barriers to entry don’t exclude people from certain socioeconomic backgrounds. Duke is the second highest-rated university in the US, and it has an acceptance rate of just 10%. This makes it one of the most selective in overall terms. However, 50% of those accepted are women and 44.5% of students are from racial-ethnic minorities. Duke’s ability to balance being highly selective with being open to students from all backgrounds comes from its fee structure. Students from wealthy backgrounds pay $76,929 for tuition. The net price for low-income families, thanks to various grants, is -$5,984. This means low-income undergraduates can not only offset their $76,929 fees, but make money thanks to grants totaling $82,913. In contrast, the lowest-scoring college by this metric, the University of Texas at Austin, has a net cost of $11,374 for low-income families. When you factor in other costs, the actual price of attending this university for a year if you’re from a low-income family is $13,677. This is mainly due to total annual grants worth $10,664, which is $72,249 less than certain students can get at Duke.

Findings

Stanford didn’t come top in either the STARS or Sierra Magazine’s Cool Schools ratings. However, it is among the top five in both, something no other university managed. For that reason, it was ranked as the most sustainable college in the US. Stanford’s Platinum STARS rating is up from Gold and is down to a significant reduction in greenhouse gas emissions. As per the university’s data, emission of greenhouse gases is down 72% on peak levels. Domestic water usage is also down 45%, while energy usage and landfill have both dropped by 26%. These results are due, in part, to operational reporting and dashboards. By installing 25+ campus dashboards alongside 135+ building specific dashboards, Stanford can monitor various sustainability metrics. These provisions sit in contrast to the relative lack of provisions at the lowest performing university, Amherst College, which currently has a Silver STARS rating.

Findings

The University of Michigan and Caltech sit at the extreme ends of the speech spectrum. The former scored 100% in our tests, while the latter had a score of 0%. This doesn’t necessarily mean that all speech is allowed at the University of Michigan and none at Caltech. However, it does suggest that freedom of speech is more of a topic at one than the other. Indeed, the University of Michigan clearly states that it is against stopping events on the grounds that some people are opposed to the content of said event. Furthermore, its Freedom of Speech and Artistic Expression policy states that “diverse points of view” are “of the highest importance” not just for the speaker, but those wanting to listen. The lowest scoring college in this category was Caltech. Although it is bound by First Amendment rights, certain internal policies mean it’s sometimes regarded as a place where free speech is stifled. For example, the Institute Policy on Unlawful Harassment makes certain forms of protected speech difficult on campus. For that reason, Caltech scored poorly in this area.

However, to provide some type of comparative analysis, we gave each institution a campus safety score. Wellesley College scored 100% thanks, in part, to its onsite police unit. That number of incidents was higher than more than 95% of US colleges, and it’s enough to make it one of the biggest crime hotspots of all the institutions included in our research.

Findings

Progressiveness ratings vary within regions, so establishing a single reason why universities in the Northeast score better, on average, than those in the South is tough. However, when you look at the top five overall performers, Ivy League leaders such as Harvard, Yale, and Cornell are all there. Moreover, they’re all located in the Northeastern region. This suggests that, in at least some way, a reputation for excellence breeds diversity and liberalism. Indeed, if colleges are more prestigious, they’re more desirable. Therefore, if said universities have mechanisms in place to allow students from all backgrounds to enroll, it makes sense they will be more diverse. When that’s true, campuses become a melting pot of ideas which, in turn, can lead to a more progressive environment. This doesn’t mean to say universities in other regions aren’t progressive. However, according to the data at least, there’s a greater focus on progressivism in Northeast Universities than elsewhere in the US.

Methodology

Diversity

When measuring diversity, we based our data on the following:

Ethnic Diversity: focuses on student and staff ethnicity, analysing the racial representation.

Gender Diversity: This is based on the ratio of male to female members within both students and staff.

Geographic Diversity: This is based on where the proportion of university intakes were from based on location.

LGBTQ Support: This is based on the LGBTQ support and organizations within each university.

Inclusivity

This is based on how available the universities were from a financial aspect, we based our data on the following:

Affordability for Low-income Families: This is based on the average net cost of the tuition fees after all the financial aid had been included for individuals who come from low-income families.

Financial Aid: This is based on financial aid in grants and scholarships that the universities offered to their undergrads on average at each listed university.

Sustainability

When looking at the sustainability of each college, we based our data on the following:

STARS: The STARS system is a reporting framework for colleges and universities to measure their sustainable performance.

Sierra Report: The Sierra report is a framework also used to analyse the sustainable performance of each university.

Using their overall score, we quantified how well each university performed based on their sustainable performance.

Freedom of Speech: This is based on the number of student organizations available at each university.

Health and Wellbeing

When looking at health and wellbeing at each college, we based our data on the following:

Campus Safety: This is based on the number of crimes committed last year on campus for each university.

Mental Health: Each university all offered a similarly high level of mental health support. With this in mind, it wasn’t included in the weighting.

The reasons for using the data sources above were the reliability, data privacy and accuracy of the findings.

Sustainability data source

One important factor to note for the sustainability performance is that many colleges release sustainability reports each year that measure the progress made in terms of the SPI, but a significant number of colleges did not release their base figures for each year.

As a result, we were unable to obtain the individual values needed to calculate the sustainable performance directly from the colleges themselves. Therefore, we have used the STARS and Sierra sustainability rankings to form our results.

Source Verification

Source data verification of each feature was conducted to ensure the data collected is reliable. This included fact checking the data presented on each website against others to ensure the data is reliable and accurate, unless the data is from the primary source. In addition to this we have ensured that the data was collected from university dedicated websites.

Scores

When analysing the data collected to calculate the scores, we have scaled all the results to between 0-100. We have categorised the features based on their groups to simplify the dataset and allow analysis by each category.

Score Weighting

When weighting the scores, it is important to keep in mind that this is a subjective approach which is based on what we deem to be important factors. We have weighted the diversity score, inclusivity score and sustainability score by 50% greater and kept the freedom of speech column and Health and Wellbeing columns their original weight of 1.
